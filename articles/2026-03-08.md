# CRISPR's Coming of Age: From a Baby's Bespoke Cure to Defeating Superbugs

In February 2025, a seven-month-old boy named KJ Muldoon became the first human being to receive a gene-editing therapy designed exclusively for him — a treatment invented, manufactured, and injected into his body in just six months. Now, in early 2026, the ripple effects of that milestone are reshaping what we expect medicine to be. Across research institutions, clinical trial registries, and hospital ICUs, CRISPR is graduating from a laboratory curiosity to a genuine force of medical transformation — one that could rewrite the terms of human disease.

## The Era of One-Size-Fits-One Medicine

KJ was born with CPS1 deficiency, a catastrophic urea-cycle disorder affecting roughly 1 in 1.3 million people. Without a functional copy of the CPS1 gene, his liver couldn't convert ammonia — a normal byproduct of protein metabolism — into urea for safe excretion. The result: toxic ammonia buildup, brain damage, and, in most cases, death.

A team at Children's Hospital of Philadelphia (CHOP) and the Perelman School of Medicine at Penn identified the precise single-letter mutation in KJ's genome and used base editing — a CRISPR variant that swaps one DNA letter without cutting the strand — to correct it. Lipid nanoparticles carried the fix directly to his liver cells. The FDA fast-tracked approval in under a week. KJ went home 307 days after he was first admitted.

"It is our goal to submit the primary IND for the UCD platform in 2026," the researchers announced, signaling plans for a Phase I/II umbrella clinical trial that could extend the treatment to other urea-cycle disorders. Nature named KJ to its list of the ten people who most shaped science in 2025. The FDA followed by opening a formal regulatory pathway — the "plausible mechanism pathway" — for bespoke personalized therapies.

The era of one-size-fits-one medicine is no longer a thought experiment.

## A New Weapon Against Superbugs

While KJ's story captured hearts, a quieter but potentially civilization-scale breakthrough emerged from UC San Diego in February 2026. Researchers led by Ethan Bier, Justin Meyer, and Victor Nizet unveiled **pPro-MobV**, a second-generation CRISPR gene drive engineered to reverse antibiotic resistance in bacterial populations.

Unlike conventional antibiotics that simply kill bacteria — and thereby accelerate Darwinian selection for resistance — pPro-MobV works by spreading a genetic "fix" *through* bacterial communities. The system targets resistance genes embedded in plasmids, small circular DNA molecules that bacteria trade promiscuously. By hitchhiking the same conjugal-transfer mechanism bacteria use to share resistance in the first place, pPro-MobV inserts itself into those plasmids and disables the resistance machinery.

Crucially, it works inside biofilms — the sticky microbial fortresses that render most antibiotics ineffective. And it includes a built-in off switch: a homology-based deletion that can remove the inserted genetic material when needed.

The stakes are immense. Antibiotic-resistant infections already kill approximately 1.27 million people per year globally; projections suggest that number could exceed 10 million annually by 2050. The work, published in *npj Antimicrobials and Resistance*, does not yet constitute a clinical treatment — but it offers a proof-of-concept that we might one day fight resistance not just defensively, but offensively, by genetically disarming pathogens in situ.

## Editing Without the Scissors

A third front is emerging that may ultimately be the most transformative: gene therapy that doesn't cut DNA at all.

Researchers at UNSW Sydney, led by Professor Merlin Crossley and collaborating with St. Jude Children's Research Hospital, demonstrated in late 2025 that CRISPR machinery can be repurposed as an epigenetic editor — stripping away methyl groups, the chemical "anchors" that silence certain genes, and reactivating them without touching the underlying DNA sequence.

"If you brush the cobwebs off, the gene comes on," Crossley explained. "These compounds aren't cobwebs — they're anchors."

One immediate application: reactivating the fetal globin gene to compensate for defective adult hemoglobin in sickle cell disease. But the broader implication is profound. Epigenetic editing sidesteps many of the safety concerns that have shadowed CRISPR since its inception — off-target cuts, chromosomal rearrangements, unintended mutations. As Crossley's co-author Professor Kate Quinlan noted, "Therapies based on this technology are likely to have a reduced risk of unintended negative effects."

## What 2026 Means for the Field

As of early 2026, approximately 250 clinical trials worldwide involve gene-editing candidates, with more than 150 currently active. CRISPR Therapeutics' CASGEVY — the first approved CRISPR therapy, targeting sickle cell disease and beta thalassemia — is expanding to younger patients aged 5–11, with global regulatory submissions expected in the first half of 2026.

What these developments collectively signal is a field moving from proof-of-concept to proof-of-practice. CRISPR is no longer asking whether it can edit human genes. It is asking how quickly, how safely, and for how many people.

KJ Muldoon's story is the most human expression of that shift — a child who should have died from a disease so rare it has no generic drug, saved by science that didn't exist for him until his doctors invented it. The tools that saved him are now being scaled, refined, and pointed at one of medicine's most entrenched adversaries: the superbug. And, quietly, a new generation of editing tools is being built that may make the original scissors-based CRISPR look crude by comparison.

The revolution is no longer coming. It arrived.
